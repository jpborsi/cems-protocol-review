
# Toxic Ingestion / Exposure / Overdose – Cardiotoxic Medications

## Overall Procedure
1. UNIVERSAL PATIENT CARE PROTOCOL
2. AIRWAY PROTOCOL / OXYGEN
3. IV / IO PROCEDURE
4. BLOOD GLUCOSE PROCEDURE  
5. TRANSPORT to appropriate facility
    1. CONTACT receiving facility
    2. CONSULT Medical Control where indicated
    3. APPROPRIATE transfer of care
6. CAPNOGRAPHY PROCEDURE  
7. DIAGNOSTIC EKG PROCEDURE and Assess Vitals
8. Treat Per Presenting EKG Rhythm

### Circulation / Shock Sub-Procedure
1. IV NORMAL SALINE BOLUS 20 ml/kg  
    - To Maintain MAP > 65 or SBP 90 if MAP Unavailable or Radial Pulses
2. Prophylactically apply pacing / defib pads and prepare for decompensation

### Cardiac Rhythm Sub-Procedure
Criteria: Select treatment based on presenting EKG rhythm and findings on EKG (QRS duration > 120ms, bradycardia, prolonged QT, etc.)

**If QRS > 120 ms (3 small EKG boxes) or findings suggesting Sodium Channel Blocker toxicity:**
1. SODIUM BICARBONATE 1 mEq/kg SLOW IV/IO
    - Until QRS narrows/condition improves
    - If no improvement, contact ONLINE MEDICAL CONTROL
    - May repeat if available and EKG changes reoccur  
2. Supportive Care
    - Therapies listed may need to all be used together in severe cases
    - Always suspect multiple substances and treat for all identified

**If Prolonged QT (>500ms, 2.5 large EKG boxes) or witnessed episodes of Torsades:**
1. MAGNESIUM SULFATE 2 grams IV/IO
    - To reduce risk of Torsades
    - Does not address Potassium blockade

**If Bradycardia:**
1. May consider adding ATROPINE 1 mg IV/IO q 3–5 mins prn (Max 3 mg)
2. TRANSCUTANEOUS PACING PROCEDURE as indicated

**For Hypotension or shock:**
1. EPINEPHrine PUSH DOSE
    - Make 10 mcg/ml
    - 10 mcg (1 ml) prn—slow push, titrate to effect
    - To Maintain MAP > 65 or SBP 90 if MAP Unavailable or Radial Pulses
    - May use up to 50 mcg (5 ml) per dose if needed

**If Calcium Channel Blocker or Beta Blocker overdose:**
1. CALCIUM CHLORIDE 1 Gram IV/IO  
   OR
2. CALCIUM GLUCONATE 1–3 Grams IV/IO  
    - May repeat either if available and EKG changes reoccur

## Indications
* Ingestion or suspected ingestion of a potentially toxic substance

## Signs and Symptoms
* Mental status changes
* Hypo/hypertension
* Decreased respiratory rate
* Tachycardia, dysrhythmias
* Seizures

## Differential Diagnosis
* Tricyclic antidepressants (TCAs)
* Acetaminophen (Tylenol)
* Depressants
* Stimulants
* Anticholinergic
* Cardiac medications
* Solvents, alcohols, cleaning agents
* Insecticides (organophosphates)
* Respiratory depression
* Other organophosphates
* Carbamates

## Key Points
* Exam: Mental Status, Skin, HEENT, Heart, Lungs, Abdomen, Extremities, Neuro
* Do not rely on patient history of ingestion, especially in suicide attempts.
* Bring bottles, contents, and emesis to ED.
* Tricyclic: 4 major areas of toxicity: seizures, dysrhythmias, hypotension, decreased mental status or coma; rapid progression from alert mental status to death.
* Acetaminophen: initially normal or nausea/vomiting. If not detected and treated, causes irreversible liver failure.
* Depressants: decreased HR, decreased BP, decreased temperature, decreased respirations, non-specific pupils.
* Stimulants: increased HR, increased BP, increased temperature, dilated pupils, and seizures.
* Anticholinergics: increased HR, increased temperature, dilated pupils, and mental status changes.
* Cardiac Medications: dysrhythmias and mental status changes.
* Solvents: nausea, vomiting, and mental status changes.
* Insecticides: increased or decreased HR, increased secretions, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, pinpoint pupils.
* Consider restraints if necessary for patient's and/or personnel's protection per the Restraint Procedure.
* If it can be done safely, take whatever container the substance came from to the hospital along with readily obtainable samples of medication unless this results in an unreasonable delay of transport.
* If applicable, DO NOT transport a patient to the hospital until properly decontaminated.
